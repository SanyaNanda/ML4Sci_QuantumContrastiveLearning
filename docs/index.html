GSoC 2024 | Learning Quantum Representations of Classical High-Energy Physics Data with Contrastive Learning

Sanya Nanda | Sept 22, 2024 | 14 min read

Google Summer of Code @ ML4Sci

This year Google celebrates its 20th anniversary of Google Summer of Code (GSoC) with 1,220 Contributors, writing code for 195 open-source mentoring organizations starting May 27, 2024. As part of GSoC 2024, I contributed to Machine Learning for Science (ML4Sci). It is an open-source organization that brings together modern machine learning techniques and applies them to cutting-edge problems in Science, Technology, Engineering, and Math (STEM). I worked in QMLHEP, where we implement Quantum Machine Learning on High-Energy Physics datasets.

Project

Learning quantum representations of classical high-energy physics data with contrastive learning:
  • Implemented a trainable embedding function to encode classical data onto a quantum model with contrastive learning.
  • Developed and experimented with different ideas for embedding functions and contrastive losses for training.
  • Benchmarked the trained embedding against a standard encoding on a given QML model

    • Background

    LHC: Large Hadron Collider

    At LHC, scientists are looking into the unknown and trying to answer the most fundamental questions about our Universe, the likes of: "What is the Universe really made of and what forces act within it?" and "What gives everything substance?" The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) is the world's largest and most powerful particle physics accelerator, located at CERN in Geneva, Switzerland. It features a 27-kilometer ring of superconducting magnets, designed to accelerate particles to speed of light and collide them to explore fundamental physics. The LHC operates at extremely low temperatures, even colder than outer space, to maintain its superconducting state. The collider has been instrumental in significant discoveries, including the Higgs boson. In total, the LHC is comprised of seven separate experiments: ALICE, LHCb, CMS, TOTEM, LHCf, MoEDAL, and ATLAS.

    CMS: Compact Muon Solenoid

    The Compact Muon Solenoid (CMS) is one of the two large general-purpose detectors located at CERN's Large Hadron Collider (LHC). It is designed to investigate a broad range of physics, from studying the Higgs boson to searching for new particles that could explain the mysteries of dark matter and other unknown phenomena. The CMS detector is massive, weighing around 14K tonnes. It is structured like a cylindrical onion, with multiple layers of detectors. These layers allow CMS to capture detailed "3D photographs" of the particle collisions occurring at the LHC, which happen at almost the speed of light. The detector uses a powerful solenoid magnet to bend the paths of charged particles, helping to determine their momentum and charge. CMS is highly sensitive, equipped to detect various particles. CMS also measures the properties of well-known particles with unprecedented precision and be on the lookout for completely new, unpredicted phenomena. Such research not only increases our understanding but may eventually spark new technologies that change the world we live in.

    1. Bending Particles

      2. A powerful magnet is needed to bend charged particles as they fly outwards from the collision point. Bending the trajectories of the particles serves two purposes:

      1. Identify the charge of the particle: positively and negatively charged particles bend in opposite directions in the same magnetic field.
      2. Measure the momentum of the particle: in an identical magnetic field, high-momentum particles bend less compared with low-momentum ones.

    2. Identifying Tracks

      3. Merely bending particles is not enough -- CMS must identify the paths taken by these bent charged particles with a very high precision. This is done by a silicon Tracker made of around 75 million individual electronic sensors arranged in concentric layers. When a charged particle flies through the Tracker layer, it interacts electromagnetically with the silicon and produces a hit -- these individual hits can then be joined together to identify the track of the traversing particle.

    3. Measuring Energy: ECAL and HCAL

      4. Information about the energies of the various particles produced in each collision is crucial to understanding what occurred at the collision point. This information is collected from two kinds of “calorimeters” in CMS. The Electromagnetic Calorimeter (ECAL) is the inner layer of the two and measures the energy of electrons and photons by stopping them completely. Hadrons, which are composite particles made up of quarks and gluons, fly through the ECAL and are stopped by the outer layer called the Hadron Calorimeter (HCAL).
